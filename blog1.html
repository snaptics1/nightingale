Who Needs a Total Knee Replacement?

Learn about the indicators and solutions for Total Knee Replacement, provided by the experts at Nightingale Hospital.

Introduction

Total Knee Replacement (TKR), also known as knee arthroplasty, is a surgical procedure designed to relieve pain and restore function in severely damaged knee joints. This treatment is highly effective for individuals who have exhausted other non-surgical methods to manage their knee pain or dysfunction. But how do you know if you or someone you know might need this procedure?

At Nightingale Hospital, we’re here to help you understand the key indicators.

Key Indicators for TKR

1. Chronic Knee Pain That Limits Daily Activities

If you experience persistent knee pain that interferes with routine activities like walking, climbing stairs, or standing for extended periods, it might be time to consult a specialist. Severe pain, especially one that disrupts your quality of life, could indicate that your knee joint is too damaged for non-surgical treatments.

2. Severe Arthritis in the Knee

The three main types of arthritis that can damage the knee joint are:

  • Osteoarthritis: Wear-and-tear damage typically caused by aging.
  • Rheumatoid Arthritis: An autoimmune condition leading to inflammation of the joint lining.
  • Post-Traumatic Arthritis: Caused by an injury that leads to long-term damage. When arthritis progresses to the point where medications, physical therapy, or injections are no longer effective, TKR may be the best solution

3. Stiffness and Limited Range of Motion

Do you struggle to bend or straighten your knee fully? A loss of mobility can significantly impact daily functions and is a common reason for considering TKR.

4. Knee Deformity

If your knee joint has become deformed, bow-legged, or knocked-knee due to arthritis or injury, surgery might be necessary to correct alignment and restore functionality.

5. Frequent Swelling and Inflammation

Persistent swelling that doesn’t respond to medications or rest is a sign of significant joint damage. Chronic inflammation can make walking and other movements painful and difficult.

6. Failure of Other Treatments

Before recommending surgery, doctors often explore non-surgical treatments, such as:

  • Pain-relief medications
  • Cortisone injections
  • Physical therapy
  • Lifestyle modifications

If these methods no longer provide relief, TKR is usually the next step.

7. Your Age and Activity Level

While TKR is common in older adults, advancements in surgical techniques have made the procedure an option for younger, active individuals with severe knee damage. The decision depends on the severity of the condition rather than age alone.
